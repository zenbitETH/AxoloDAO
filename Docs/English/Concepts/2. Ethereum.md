# Ethereum

Ethereum is a decentralized blockchain network and open-source software platform powered by its native digital asset, Ether (`ETH`). Since its launch in 2015, Ethereum has evolved from an experimental blockchain into one of the core infrastructures of the decentralized digital economy. The system operates as a global distributed ledger maintained by thousands of independent computers (nodes) that collectively validate transactions and maintain a shared state without relying on a central authority. Through its programmable architecture, Ethereum enables the creation of decentralized applications (dApps), digital assets, and financial protocols that operate autonomously through smart contracts. 

## ⛛ 1. The 3 Ethereum Resources
For analytical clarity, Ethereum can be understood through three complementary dimensions: **a decentralized network**, **a financial asset (ETH)**, and **a programmable software platform** that enables an entire ecosystem of decentralized technologies.

### A. Ethereum as a Decentralized Network
Ethereum functions as a global, public, and censorship-resistant distributed ledger that operates through a peer-to-peer network of independent computers known as nodes. These nodes collectively validate transactions, execute smart contracts, and maintain the integrity of the blockchain without relying on centralized intermediaries such as banks, corporations, or governments. Because the network is distributed across thousands of geographic locations, Ethereum provides strong resilience against censorship, single points of failure, and regulatory shutdowns. 

Participation in the network is permissionless—anyone can run a node, validate transactions, or interact with applications built on the protocol. This architecture enables Ethereum to serve as the base layer for a decentralized economy where digital trust is enforced by cryptography and consensus rather than institutional authority.The resilience and neutrality of the Ethereum network are strengthened by its global node distribution. As of 2026, more than fourteen thousand active nodes maintain the network across dozens of countries, with major concentrations in the United States, Germany, and Japan.

Ethereum’s protocol is organized into two coordinated layers: the **execution layer (EL)**, where transactions and smart contracts are processed, and the **consensus layer (CL)**, where Proof of Stake validators coordinate fork choice and finality. In production, these layers run as separate client software connected through APIs, with distinct peer-to-peer networking responsibilities.

Its design philosophy emphasizes **simplicity**, **universality**, and **modularity**: the protocol aims to expose low-level primitives (instead of hard-coding narrow features), keep complexity encapsulated where possible, and remain adaptable through open upgrades (EIPs). Ethereum also follows a neutrality principle, prioritizing permissionless usage and protocol-level incentives over application-level discrimination.

#### 🌱 Environmental footprint
A key milestone in Ethereum's evolution was **The Merge** in September 2022, which transitioned the protocol from the energy‑intensive Proof of Work (PoW) consensus mechanism to a significantly more efficient Proof of Stake (PoS) model. This upgrade reduced Ethereum’s energy consumption by more than **99.95%** and notably lowered the network’s environmental footprint.

Prior to the Merge, a single Ethereum transaction required as much energy as the average American household uses in a week, producing a carbon footprint of **109.71 kg of CO₂**. Following the transition, the carbon footprint of a single transaction dropped to approximately **0.01 kg of CO₂**. This shift addressed one of the most persistent environmental criticisms of blockchain infrastructure and repositioned Ethereum as a sustainable settlement layer for global digital finance.

In its post‑Merge state, the Ethereum network operates with an energy demand comparable to that of a **small data center rather than a small country**. Estimates from the Cambridge Centre for Alternative Finance (CCAF) indicate an annualized energy consumption of roughly **7.36–7.50 GWh**, equivalent to the yearly electricity usage of about **2,000 UK households**.

Annual greenhouse gas emissions have also declined dramatically. Current estimates place Ethereum’s emissions at approximately **2.6–2.8 kilotonnes of CO₂ equivalent (KtCO₂e)** per year. For context, this is comparable to the emissions generated by roughly **five round‑trip flights between London and New York**. In addition, the network’s energy mix is becoming progressively cleaner; by late 2025, around **48% of the electricity used by Ethereum nodes** originated from low‑carbon sources, including **renewables (32%) and nuclear energy (16%)**.

### B. Ethereum as a Software Development Platform
Ethereum functions as an open-source programmable infrastructure often described as a **"world computer."** Developers deploy smart contracts to the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM), where contract code executes deterministically and state transitions are verified by the network. This model enables trust-minimized applications across decentralized finance (DeFi), stablecoins, tokenization, identity, gaming, social protocols, and DAO governance.

From a builder perspective, Ethereum development is centered on four operational primitives:
- **Transactions and gas:** every state-changing action consumes gas, and users pay fees in ETH. Gas pricing combines a network base fee with optional priority fees, so application UX must account for variable execution cost and transaction confirmation time.
- **Wallets and accounts:** wallets are the user interface to cryptographic accounts that sign transactions and control assets. Externally owned accounts (EOAs), smart contract accounts, multisigs, and account-abstraction patterns each change how authentication, recovery, and permissions are designed.
- **Token and contract standards:** common interfaces (such as ERC-20 and ERC-721) make assets and applications interoperable across wallets, exchanges, bridges, and dApps, allowing developers to compose existing infrastructure instead of rebuilding it.
- **Events and indexing:** contracts emit logs (events) that power analytics, notifications, and historical application views. Production systems usually pair onchain contracts with indexers so frontend apps can query activity efficiently.

Ethereum’s software platform now operates as a multi-layer execution environment. The base layer prioritizes decentralization and security, while Layer 2 networks execute transactions at lower cost and higher throughput, then settle to Ethereum for finality. In practice, modern dApps are built as **onchain systems spanning L1 + L2**, where developers choose networks based on cost, liquidity, distribution, and ecosystem fit.

This architecture supports a large developer community and millions of daily transactions across the Ethereum ecosystem. The result is a mature platform where teams can ship globally accessible applications with shared standards, portable tooling, and security anchored to Ethereum’s settlement layer. As of early 2026, Ethereum’s software platform is defined first by performance at two layers: secure settlement on L1 and high-throughput execution on L2s.

### C. Ethereum as an Asset (ETH)
`ETH` is the native digital asset of the Ethereum network and acts as its core economic engine. Every interaction on Ethereum—from sending tokens to executing complex smart contracts—requires the payment of **gas**, a transaction fee denominated in ETH that compensates validators for processing and securing network activity. Beyond its operational role, ETH has also emerged as a global financial asset held by individuals, institutions, and corporate treasuries. The asset serves multiple economic functions: it is used for transaction fees, staking collateral in the Proof of Stake consensus system, and as a store of value within decentralized financial ecosystems. As Ethereum adoption has expanded, ETH has increasingly been treated as both a productive asset and a reserve asset within the broader crypto economy.

#### Suply Dynamics
Ethereum’s economic model combines issuance and scarcity mechanisms. Validators earn newly issued ETH as rewards for securing the network through staking, while a portion of every transaction fee is permanently removed from circulation through the **EIP‑1559 burn mechanism**. This dynamic creates the possibility of supply contraction during periods of high network activity.

As of early 2026, Ethereum mainnet secures approximately **$260 billion**, representing the cumulative value of assets, contracts, and tokenized activity that depend on Ethereum’s settlement and security guarantees.

## 2. Web2 to Web3 Shift (Conceptual Comparison)
Ethereum is often framed as the foundational infrastructure enabling the transition from the centralized internet (Web2) to a decentralized and ownership‑based internet commonly referred to as **Web3**. In the Web2 model, digital services are typically controlled by centralized platforms that store user data on proprietary servers and monetize it through advertising or subscription models. Web3 introduces a different paradigm in which identity, data, and digital assets are controlled directly by users through cryptographic wallets and decentralized protocols. Ethereum provides the infrastructure for this shift by enabling programmable ownership, decentralized identity through systems like ENS, and permissionless financial and social applications.

- **Identity:** transitions from platform-controlled accounts (email and centralized logins) to wallet-based identity and ENS domains controlled directly by users.
- **Security:** shifts from trust in centralized intermediaries to cryptographic verification and self-custody of assets.
- **Language layer:** evolves from the "online/cloud" model to **onchain execution**, where application logic is enforced by blockchain protocols.
- **Service access:** moves from centralized servers to distributed networks of nodes that maintain shared state.
- **Business models:** transition from subscription and advertising models toward ownership-driven ecosystems built around tokens, decentralized applications (dApps), and DAOs.
