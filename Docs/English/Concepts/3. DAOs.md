# DAOs

## 1. What is a DAO?
A decentralized autonomous organization, or DAO, is an internet-native organization that coordinates people, capital, and decision-making through shared rules, usually encoded in smart contracts and governance processes on a blockchain.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup> 

In practice, a DAO is rarely "fully autonomous" in the literal sense; it is better understood as a community and treasury organized around transparent rules, onchain records, and open participation rather than a traditional corporate hierarchy.<sup>[2](https://blog.ethereum.org/2014/05/06/daos-dacs-das-and-more-an-incomplete-terminology-guide)</sup> On Ethereum and Ethereum-aligned ecosystems, DAOs are one of the main institutional forms of Web3 because they combine programmable ownership, shared treasury management, and collective governance. Members can propose actions, debate them in public, delegate voting power, and fund work from a common treasury without relying on a single company to own the system end-to-end.<sup>[3](https://paragraph.com/@lindaxie/a-beginner-s-guide-to-daos)</sup>

## 2. Background
The DAO concept emerged from early Ethereum thinking about organizations that could operate through software-defined rules instead of conventional legal and managerial structures. In 2014, Vitalik Buterin described a broader vocabulary around decentralized organizations, distinguishing between decentralized autonomous organizations, decentralized autonomous corporations, and related forms in order to show that "DAO" was one point in a wider design space rather than a single fixed template.<sup>[2](https://blog.ethereum.org/2014/05/06/daos-dacs-das-and-more-an-incomplete-terminology-guide)</sup>

The term became widely known in 2016 with **The DAO**, a large experimental venture fund built on Ethereum. Its smart contract exploit and the resulting Ethereum fork became a foundational governance lesson: DAOs can coordinate capital at internet scale, but they also introduce new security, legitimacy, and recovery challenges.<sup>[3](https://paragraph.com/@lindaxie/a-beginner-s-guide-to-daos)</sup> Since then, DAOs have evolved from simple token-voting experiments into a broad family of institutions governing protocols, grants, ecosystems, NFT communities, public goods, and shared treasuries.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup>

## 3. Differences between a DAO and a company
A company is normally organized through legal incorporation, executive management, private ownership records, and internal decision-making processes. A DAO, by contrast, tends to make its treasury, governance rules, and proposal history legible to the public, with key decisions executed or recorded onchain.<sup>[3](https://paragraph.com/@lindaxie/a-beginner-s-guide-to-daos)</sup> This does not mean DAOs have no leadership; it means leadership is usually more explicit, more contestable, and more tightly coupled to transparent governance processes.

The most important difference is where trust sits. In a company, users and workers typically trust directors, managers, and legal enforcement. In a DAO, participants rely more heavily on smart contracts, visible governance rules, public treasury records, and delegated or token-based representation.<sup>[2](https://blog.ethereum.org/2014/05/06/daos-dacs-das-and-more-an-incomplete-terminology-guide)</sup> The tradeoff is structural: DAOs can be more open and censorship-resistant, but they are often slower, noisier, and more exposed to governance capture, voter apathy, and unclear accountability if they are badly designed.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup>

| Aspect | DAO | Company |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | 🌎 Fully global | 🏘️ Local |
| Decision-making | 🔻 Bottom-up | 🔺 Top-down |
| Structure | 🔗 Onchain bounded | 📜 Legally bounded |
| Finance | ⛲ Decentralized finance | 🏦 Traditional finance |
| Coordination | 🪞 Transparent coordination | 🕶️ Private coordination |

## 4. Types and sizes of DAOs
DAOs now cover several major categories. Some are **protocol DAOs**, which govern DeFi or infrastructure systems such as DEXs and lending markets. Others are **ecosystem and grants DAOs**, which allocate capital to public goods, community growth, or research. There are also **investment DAOs**, **collector DAOs**, and **service DAOs**, each with different treasury logic and participation norms.<sup>[3](https://paragraph.com/@lindaxie/a-beginner-s-guide-to-daos)</sup>

| Type of DAO | Core function | Typical outputs | Primary Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| ⚙️ Protocol DAO | Govern a live protocol or network | Parameter changes, treasury decisions, upgrades | Uniswap, Aave, MakerDAO (Sky), Curve, Compound, Lido |
| 🌱 Grants / Public Goods DAO | Allocate funds to ecosystem contributors | Grants, retro funding, ecosystem support | Gitcoin, ENS, Arbitrum Foundation (grant arms) |
| 💼 Investment DAO  | Pool capital for collective allocation | Portfolio decisions, investment mandates | MetaCartel Ventures, The LAO, Orange DAO |
| 🖼️ Collector DAO  | Coordinate cultural or NFT acquisition | Shared collections, curation, community ownership | Flamingo DAO, PleasrDAO, Fingerprints DAO |
| 🛠️ Service DAO  | Organize contributors around deliverables | Design, development, operations, research | Llama, Vector DAO, Blocsys, Blocktunix |

"DAO" is not a single institutional template but a broad category of internet-native organizations with different purposes. Protocol DAOs govern live systems and financial infrastructure; grants and public goods DAOs allocate capital to ecosystems; investment DAOs coordinate pooled capital; collector DAOs organize cultural ownership; and service DAOs coordinate contributors around deliverables. The key analytical point is that DAO design should be evaluated against function. The right governance process, treasury structure, and accountability model depend on whether the organization is operating a protocol, funding a community, managing assets, curating culture, or delivering work.

DAO scale is multidimensional rather than reducible to a single leaderboard. Some DAOs are large because they govern major protocols with substantial TVL, such as Lido, Aave, Uniswap, and Sky; others are large because they control significant treasuries, such as Optimism, Arbitrum, Mantle, and ENS. Token-holder counts add a different signal again, showing breadth of participation rather than economic weight. In practice, scale can mean network usage, treasury depth, or governance reach depending on the type of organization being measured.

## 5. How to participate in DAOs
Participating in a DAO usually begins well before voting. Entry into DAO life is less about formal employment and more about learning how a community coordinates work, capital, and decision-making in public.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup> New participants typically begin by reading the forum, joining community calls, understanding the mission, reviewing previous proposals, and identifying who is actually doing the work. In many DAOs, token ownership or delegated voting power matters, but meaningful participation often starts through discussion, research, operations, grants review, or contribution to working groups rather than through capital alone.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/) , </sup><sup>[3](https://paragraph.com/@lindaxie/a-beginner-s-guide-to-daos)</sup>

There are several common participation paths: holding and voting a governance token, delegating to a trusted representative, serving as a delegate, contributing labor to a working group, applying for grants, or proposing improvements directly.<sup>[3](https://paragraph.com/@lindaxie/a-beginner-s-guide-to-daos)</sup> The practical standard is to begin narrow, follow one domain, understand the incentives, and build credibility through consistent contribution. DAOs reward context more than raw opinion, and healthy participation depends on members who understand both the mission and the tradeoffs behind proposals.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup>

### How to join a DAO
DAOs are globally accessible organizations: anyone with internet access can, in principle, participate without the geographic and bureaucratic restrictions that shape many traditional institutions.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup> In practice, however, open access does not remove the need for preparation. New participants benefit from understanding the basics of Web3, blockchain infrastructure, wallets, tokens, and decentralized governance before trying to contribute in a meaningful way.

Entry paths vary significantly from one DAO to another. Some require specific governance tokens to vote or access certain rights, while others allow open participation through forums, working groups, Discord servers, or formal contributor interviews.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup> The underlying principle is that coordination happens through communities, not only through software. Sustainable DAO growth depends on people building trust, sharing context, and organizing around common goals rather than merely appearing onchain.

### How to use DAOs to coordinate resources and solve problems
DAOs can function as coordination tools for allocating resources and solving problems at global scale.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup> Through community voting, public proposals, and smart-contract-based execution, they can move capital and authority with a level of transparency and programmability that is difficult to reproduce inside conventional bureaucratic systems. This makes them especially useful where open participation, auditable decisions, and cross-border collaboration matter.

This coordination model is also relevant to intellectual property, digital rights, and the funding of new initiatives. DAOs can help distribute ownership, govern shared creative assets, and direct financing toward projects that align with the values of their participants. In that sense, they do not only replace intermediaries; they also change who gets to decide, who gets access to capital, and which kinds of projects can be supported. Their broader promise is to lower barriers to innovation by democratizing access to resources that were historically concentrated in firms, financial institutions, or small founding teams.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup>

### How to use DAOs to get paid
DAOs also create new income models for contributors. Some organizations compensate participants in ETH or stable-value tokens, while others pay in community-native tokens whose value depends on the DAO's broader economic health.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup> This opens different paths to earning: building a career inside Web3, contributing to specific protocols or communities, or using DAO work as a supplementary income stream.

At the same time, compensation in DAOs carries structural tradeoffs. A DAO token can appreciate if the organization develops durable revenue, treasury strength, or network demand, but it can also expose contributors to volatility and sustainability risk. Long-term uncertainty remains significant, especially around regulation, treasury management, and whether a DAO's economic model can support stable compensation over time.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup>

Several payment models are common across DAOs:

| 💸 Payment Model | Description |
|---|---|
| 🎯 Bounty System | Crypto rewards for completing specific tasks or projects such as development, marketing, research, or community management |
| 🔗 Protocol-Based Rewards | Rewards tied to participation in a specific blockchain protocol, often through staking, governance, liquidity, or usage incentives |
| 📅 Fixed Payment or Salary | Recurring compensation for ongoing roles, often subject to community approval or governance voting |
| 🗳️ Community Distribution | Contributor compensation allocated collectively through community review and voting |

### How to use DAOs to join social clubs
Not all DAOs are organized primarily around finance or protocol governance. Social DAOs are centered on connection among people who share interests, values, and cultural commitments.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup> These organizations function as decentralized clubs where members learn together, collaborate on projects, and build identity through participation rather than through a conventional membership institution.

NFT-based DAOs are one important branch of this social form. Some form around specific collections, others around decentralized curation across multiple works, and others around supporting artistic production under a shared aesthetic or cultural identity. In each case, the DAO becomes a coordination layer for community, ownership, and meaning, not only a treasury or voting machine.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup>

| 🌐 Participation Path | Core Use | Typical Mechanism | Main Tradeoff |
|---|---|---|---|
| 🚪 Joining a DAO | Enter a global, internet-native organization | Forums, Discord, calls, token access, contributor onboarding | Openness still requires learning and context |
| 🧭 Coordinating with DAOs | Allocate resources and solve shared problems | Proposals, voting, smart contracts, transparent treasury flows | Efficiency depends on governance design and execution quality |
| 💰 Getting paid through DAOs | Earn income from contribution or protocol participation | Bounties, salaries, protocol rewards, community distributions | Compensation can be volatile or economically uncertain |
| 🎨 Joining social DAOs | Build community around shared interests, culture, or identity | NFT membership, curation, collaboration, collective ownership | Social cohesion matters as much as token mechanics |

## 6. How to create DAOs
Creating a DAO is not mainly a token launch problem; it is an institutional design problem. A credible DAO needs a clear mission, a narrow scope of decisions, transparent treasury processes, and explicit rules about who can propose, who can decide, and how failure is handled.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup> Good DAO design also makes power legible rather than pretending it does not exist: token holders, delegates, contributors, councils, and foundations should each have publicly defined roles, and authority should be revocable, bounded, and matched to the kind of decision being made.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup>

Operationally, most DAOs combine onchain and offchain coordination. Treasuries, voting, and binding commitments may live onchain or be anchored to onchain systems, while discussion, contributor coordination, and fast-moving workflows often remain offchain for usability. The central design challenge is to build systems that are resilient, transparent, and scalable without concentrating too much power in a few people or asking the full DAO to make every specialized decision directly.<sup>[1](https://howtodao.xyz/)</sup><sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup>

### DAO Design Principles

### 🔢 Use magic numbers for designing teams
Team size should stay deliberately small. A practical target is roughly 7 to 14 people, because beyond that coordination quality tends to fall and discussion becomes harder to manage.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> There also appears to be an upper limit of roughly 50 full-time or near-full-time contributors before a DAO needs more formal subdivision, which implies only a limited number of councils can be supported effectively. In practice, many DAOs converge on about five critical business functions, such as technical evaluation, treasury management, finance, grants, and governance operations.

### 🏛️ Define power structures
Power structures cannot be avoided, so they should be defined openly rather than ignored. If it is unclear who has authority, who is responsible, or who holds expertise, the community will often resolve that ambiguity through informal political struggle, which creates social friction and weak accountability.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> Leadership roles should therefore be visible, flexible, and revocable, whether they are informal signals of trust or formal elected positions. When expertise matters, experts should either be empowered to decide or made clearly identifiable so non-experts can rely on them.

### ⚖️ Delegate convex decisions, make concave decisions directly
Not every decision should be pushed to the full DAO. Specialized or "convex" decisions, where quality depends heavily on expertise and poor compromise can create bad outcomes, are better delegated to smaller expert groups such as councils or boards.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> Examples include evaluating grant applications or setting technical milestones. By contrast, the full DAO should retain direct control over broader "concave" decisions such as approving budgets, electing candidates, or ratifying high-level frameworks. Councils are decision-making bodies; boards are advisory bodies used when specific expertise is needed.

### 🗳️ Distinguish between representatives and contributors
DAOs should separate roles that exercise public authority from roles that primarily perform work. Representatives hold delegated rights that token holders would otherwise exercise, or they occupy roles that a foundation would otherwise perform, so they should usually be elected and held accountable as public officials.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> Contributors, by contrast, mainly execute work and should not automatically be subject to the same election logic or public scrutiny.

### 🔄 Design for resilience to churn, not resistance to it
Churn is normal in DAOs because many contributors and delegates operate in flexible, part-time environments. The goal is not to prevent turnover at all costs, but to ensure that no single person becomes a point of failure.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> Team leads should coordinate and enable others rather than monopolize strategy or decision-making, power should be distributed across many contributors, and onboarding systems should be strong enough that high-context roles can be replaced quickly when needed.

### 🤝 Avoid the principal-agent problem
DAO structures should minimize "us versus them" dynamics between token holders, core teams, service providers, and community initiatives. Principal-agent problems often emerge when one group controls budgets or execution on behalf of another without aligned incentives, especially in waterfall-style compensation schemes.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> Teams should have as much autonomy as possible over their own operations and budgets, and community-led initiatives should not depend unnecessarily on core teams for execution.

### 📊 Make data driven decisions
DAO governance should reduce the number of subjective judgments that voters must make directly. Purely subjective systems often produce weak outcomes, but fully removing humans from decisions can make a DAO rigid and slow.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> A better approach is to use more objective frameworks, smart defaults, and algorithmic structures where possible, while preserving human oversight for exceptions, overrides, and higher-level judgment. This is especially important in treasury allocation, where subjective bias often undermines protocol-level outcomes.

### 🎯 Value outputs, not inputs
DAOs should focus less on supporting vaguely defined teams and more on funding clearly scoped initiatives with measurable outputs. Working groups frequently underperform when mandates are broad and work is hard to evaluate.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> Decision-making about what should be done should be separated from decision-making about who should do it, and compensation should be tied as much as possible to output and impact rather than hours worked or generalized activity.

### 🧩 Redundancy creates waste, not competition
Creating multiple internal teams with overlapping mandates rarely produces healthy competition inside a DAO. More often it creates fragmented ownership, inefficient handoffs, governance friction, and a confusing experience for contributors and users.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> External competition usually works better, especially through competitive RFP processes where service providers compete to deliver clearly scoped initiatives. Even then, mission-critical deliverables and core strategic advantages should not depend entirely on any one team.

### 📏 Scope should be narrow, not broad
Most DAO teams fail because their mandates are too broad. Broad scopes blur accountability, invite waste, and often create room for ineffective budgeting or outright abuse.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> Meta-workstreams and cross-functional structures also tend to add coordination overhead in environments where coordination is already expensive. Narrow scopes, mandates, and RFPs make work easier to evaluate, resource, and govern.

### 🏗️ Scale via public goods rather than public services
Shared services across an entire DAO often suffer from diseconomies of scale because different teams have different needs and the usual accountability mechanisms are weak. Public infrastructure tends to scale better than public service provision.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> For example, a team that builds reusable data infrastructure for everyone is often more effective than a team trying to provide customized analytics as a service across the whole DAO.

### 🧭 Achieve alignment through action not artifacts
Strategic alignment should be built into operations rather than added later through documents, frameworks, or bureaucratic artifacts. Many DAOs first create structures with unclear purpose and then try to retroactively align them with strategy through more paperwork.<sup>[4](https://plaid-cement-e44.notion.site/The-Collective-DAO-Archives-dc3659bbebf248ffa56190c0394b7540)</sup> A stronger model is to design processes so that acting within the system is already aligned by default, for example by forcing proposals to compete under a goal-constrained budget rather than merely claiming to support a general objective.
