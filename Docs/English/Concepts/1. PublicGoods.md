# Public Goods

A **public good** is a resource or service that is accessible to everyone without meaningful restriction, and whose use by one person does not significantly reduce its availability to others. These goods typically exhibit **non-rivalry** and **non-excludability**, meaning they can be widely shared and are difficult or costly to restrict to paying users.


## 📘 1. Core definition
A **pure public good** has two properties:
- **Non-rivalry**: one person using it does not reduce others' ability to use it.
- **Non-excludability**: it is hard or costly to exclude non-payers.

This is the classical framing from Samuelson (1954).<sup>[1](https://doi.org/10.2307/1925895)</sup>

## 🧩 2. Public goods vs related categories
| Type | Rivalry | Excludability | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Private good | Rival | Excludable | Food, laptops |
| Club good | Non-rival (until congestion) | Excludable | Subscription platforms |
| Common-pool resource (CPR) | Rival | Non-excludable (or hard to exclude) | Fisheries, groundwater |
| Pure public good | Non-rival | Non-excludable | Clean air, basic internet protocols |

Important distinction (Ostrom): many natural resources are **CPRs**, not pure public goods.<sup>[2](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2009/ostrom/lecture/)</sup>

## ⚙️ 3. Main structural challenges
Public goods are underprovided by markets because of:
- **Free-rider problem**: people benefit without paying.
- **Positive externalities**: society captures far more value than producers can monetize.
- **Tragedy of the commons** (especially for CPRs): overuse/depletion without governance.
- **Coordination and time-horizon failures**: benefits are long-term, costs are immediate.

## 🌍 4. Tangible, intangible, and international public goods
### Tangible public goods (mostly physical)
- Street lighting
- Public roads (up to congestion limits)
- Flood-control systems
- Clean air systems and public sanitation infrastructure

### Intangible public goods (knowledge/protocol/institutional)
- Scientific knowledge
- Open standards and internet base protocols (TCP/IP, DNS, HTTP)
- Open educational content
- Open-source software components widely reused by society

### International / global public goods (IPGs/GPGs)
Benefits cross borders and generations:
- Climate stability
- Ozone layer protection
- Biodiversity conservation
- Pandemic prevention/surveillance knowledge

From the international public goods literature, these are still public goods in economic logic, but governance is harder because no single world government can enforce full contribution.

## 🌿 5. Why natural resources need governance
Natural resources split into two groups:
- **CPRs** (fisheries, forests, aquifers): non-excludable + rival -> vulnerable to overuse.
- **Global ecological public goods** (stable climate, ozone): non-rival + non-excludable at planetary level.

Inference from sources: treating all natural systems as ordinary private assets creates systematic depletion risk; durable management requires institutions, rules, monitoring, and collective enforcement.

## 💻 6. Why OSS should be considered a public good (or near-public digital infrastructure)
OSS has core public-good dynamics:
- Code is **non-rival** (copying is near-zero marginal cost).
- Open licenses reduce exclusion barriers.
- The ecosystem creates large spillovers for firms, governments, and education.<sup>[3](https://www.fordfoundation.org/learning/library/research-reports/roads-and-bridges-the-unseen-labor-behind-our-digital-infrastructure/)</sup>

But OSS has a funding paradox:
- Usage scales quickly; maintenance labor does not.
- Critical dependencies are often maintained by small teams.
- Free-riding is common, so maintenance is underfunded.

This is why grants, sponsorships, foundations, and shared financing models are needed.

## ⛓️ 7. Why Ethereum should be considered a public good
Best interpretation: Ethereum is **public infrastructure with mixed-good edges**.<sup>[4](https://ethereum.org/en/glossary/)</sup>

Public-good side:
- Open/permissionless participation at protocol level.
- Shared settlement and verification infrastructure used by many actors.
- Large positive externalities: DeFi, stablecoins, DAOs, identity, tokenization build on top.

Mixed-good caveat:
- Blockspace is scarce in short windows (so transaction inclusion is rival and priced by gas).
- Therefore Ethereum is not a perfectly pure public good at every layer.

Why the public-good framing still holds:
- The base trust infrastructure behaves like internet protocols: broad enablement, low direct value capture relative to downstream value created.
- If core maintenance (clients, research, security, tooling) is underfunded, systemic fragility rises.
