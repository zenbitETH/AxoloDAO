## Online(Web2) and Onchain(Web3)
The distinction between **online/cloud** and **onchain/blockchain** paradigms can be understood as a structural difference between the dominant architecture of **Web2** and the emerging architecture of **Web3**. In Web2, services are generally organized around client-server and cloud-based models in which platforms host applications, store user data in proprietary databases, and govern access through centralized interfaces. In Web3, by contrast, core records, asset ownership, and increasingly application logic are anchored **onchain**, meaning that execution, verification, and state transitions are mediated by blockchain protocols rather than by a single provider. This transition is not only a technical change in stack design, but also a redistribution of control over identity, coordination, and value creation.

From the perspective of digital political economy, Web2 expanded participation in the internet by enabling social interaction, creator platforms, and large-scale cloud services, yet it also concentrated power in a small set of intermediaries that own the infrastructure, govern the rules of access, and monetize user data. Web3 reframes this structure around decentralization, transparency, and user-controlled assets. In this sense, **online** and **cloud** are best associated with the service logic of Web2, while **onchain** is best associated with the ownership, verification, and coordination logic of Web3. Ethereum plays a central role in this shift because it generalizes blockchain infrastructure beyond payments, enabling programmable assets, decentralized identity primitives, smart contracts, and public execution environments that support dApps, DAOs, public goods funding, and open coordination.

## 1. Structural Differences Between Web2 and Web3
The clearest way to understand the shift from online to onchain systems is to identify how Web2 and Web3 differ across identity, access, security, language, and business models. These differences reflect distinct assumptions about who controls data, who enforces rules, and how value circulates through the network.

| Topic | Web2 (The Social Web) | Web3 (The Ownership Web) |
|---|---|---|
| 🪪 Identity | Email address, web domains, centralized login | Encrypted address, ENS domains, wallet-based |
| ✅ Access | Concentrated in servers operated by intermediaries | Distributed in nodes without intermediaries |
| 🛡️ Security | Dependent on the intermediary/central server | Dependent on the user (private keys/cryptography) |
| 🌐 Language | Online, cloud-based, centralized cloud | Onchain, blockchain, decentralized ledger |
| 📈 Business Model | Subscriptions, ad-based, data-renting | Ownership-based, protocols, dApps, DAOs |

### 🪪 Identity
In the Web2 environment, identity is fragmented across services and mediated by centralized providers. Users typically authenticate through email addresses, usernames, passwords, and web-domain-based accounts, while the platform remains the effective custodian of the user's digital persona. This model makes identity portable only at the discretion of the provider and often ties reputation, communication, and access rights to a corporate database. As a result, users must trust intermediaries to store personal information securely and govern its use responsibly.

In Web3, identity is redefined through cryptographic control and wallet-based presence. Ethereum addresses, digital signatures, and naming systems such as the **Ethereum Name Service (ENS)** allow users to manage identity through keys they control directly. This model aligns with the broader notion of **self-sovereign identity**, where the user, rather than the platform, becomes the primary holder of credentials, reputation, and access rights. Within Ethereum ecosystems, this shift also enables more portable social and organizational coordination: the same wallet can interact across dApps, DAOs, DeFi protocols, NFT systems, and onchain communities without requiring the recreation of identity from scratch.

### ✅ Access
Web2 access is organized around centralized servers and intermediary-controlled gateways. Platforms can suspend accounts, restrict content visibility, change terms of service, throttle APIs, or remove data with little user recourse. This makes service availability highly dependent on the operational and political decisions of platform operators.

By contrast, Web3 services are distributed across networks of nodes that replicate and verify shared state. In an onchain environment, participation is **permissionless** at the protocol layer: users do not need a company's approval to hold assets, deploy contracts, or interact with decentralized applications. Ethereum exemplifies this model by exposing a neutral execution layer where transactions and smart contracts can be validated by the network rather than by a proprietary backend. This property is especially important for public-goods-oriented infrastructures, because it reduces dependence on institutional gatekeepers and makes shared systems more resistant to censorship, arbitrary exclusion, and unilateral rule changes.

### 🛡️ Security
Security in Web2 is primarily based on trust in institutions. Users rely on a company to secure databases, recover accounts, detect intrusions, and remediate breaches. While this model can be convenient, it also creates concentrated attack surfaces: a compromise of one provider can expose data and credentials for millions of users simultaneously.

Web3 shifts security toward cryptographic verification and user custody. Assets and permissions are controlled by private keys, and transaction validity is enforced by network consensus and smart-contract logic rather than by a central administrator. Ethereum operationalizes this model through signed transactions, account abstraction research, programmable access control, and composable contract systems. This architecture reduces reliance on trusted intermediaries, but it also increases the burden on users and organizations: loss of keys, poor wallet hygiene, or insecure smart contracts can lead to irreversible failures. The Web3 security model therefore replaces institutional dependency with cryptographic sovereignty, but not without significant operational responsibility.

### 🌐 Language & culture
The technical language of Web2 is rooted in concepts such as **online services**, **cloud hosting**, **platform backends**, and **client-server architecture**. In this environment, application logic is commonly executed offchain on privately controlled infrastructure, and users access those services through browsers, apps, and APIs.

The language of Web3 is instead centered on **onchain execution**, **blockchain state**, **smart contracts**, and **decentralized ledgers**. Here, the ledger is not merely a storage layer but an execution environment where rules can be enforced transparently and shared across participants. Ethereum is the canonical example of this paradigm because it introduced a general-purpose smart-contract platform in which code can define asset ownership, governance processes, financial primitives, attestations, and coordination mechanisms. For projects concerned with open coordination and public goods, this matters because onchain systems make rules inspectable, state transitions auditable, and collective action legible at the protocol level.

### 📈 Business Models
Web2 business models are predominantly organized around subscriptions, advertising, platform fees, and the extraction or rental of user data. The platform captures the majority of network effects because it owns the interface, the database, and the monetization channels.

Web3 introduces an ownership-oriented model in which users, developers, and communities can coordinate around tokens, shared treasuries, governance rights, and protocol-level incentives. This does not automatically eliminate concentration, but it changes the locus of value creation. Ethereum's programmable asset model enables digital property, tokenized coordination, and decentralized finance, while DAOs and public goods funding mechanisms extend this logic into governance and collective resource allocation. In that sense, the move from online to onchain is also a move from platform dependency toward protocol-mediated coordination.

## 2. How Online and Onchain Features Complement Each Other
Although Web2 and Web3 are often presented as opposites, in practice their features are complementary when system designers are explicit about what belongs offchain and what must remain onchain. Cloud infrastructure remains highly effective for low-latency interfaces, media delivery, analytics, search, filtering, personalization, and mainstream onboarding. Onchain systems, by contrast, are strongest where verifiability, asset ownership, transparency, shared execution, and credible neutrality matter most. For this reason, many emerging systems adopt hybrid architectures in which cloud services handle usability and performance, while blockchains secure identity, governance, attestations, assets, and critical state transitions.

This complementarity can be clarified through an **onchain litmus test**. Functions should be placed onchain when they require **trustless ownership** (for example, who owns a token, NFT, or position), **trustless exchange** (swapping, lending, borrowing, or trading), **composability** (other contracts need to call the logic directly), **censorship resistance** (the system must still work even if the original team disappears), or **permanent commitments** such as votes, attestations, and proofs. By contrast, functions should remain offchain when they are primarily about user profiles, preferences, search, sorting, media hosting, frequently changing business logic, or any process that does not fundamentally depend on trustless value transfer or verification.

| 🧭 Litmus Test Area | ⛓️ Put It Onchain | ☁️ Keep It Offchain |
|---|---|---|
| 👑 Ownership | Trustless ownership of tokens, NFTs, and positions | User profiles, preferences, and settings |
| 💱 Exchange | Swapping, trading, lending, and borrowing | Search, filtering, and sorting |
| 🧩 Composability | Other contracts need to call the logic directly | Images, videos, and metadata storage |
| 🚫 Censorship | Must keep working even if the original team disappears | Frequently changing business logic |
| 🗳️ Commitments | Votes, attestations, and proofs | Flows that do not require trustless value transfer or verification |
| ⚖️ Reputation | Onchain commitments such as hashes or attestations | Offchain computation of reputation scores |
| 📊 Activity Feeds | Onchain events as the source of truth | Offchain indexing and feed generation |
| 📈 Price Data | Oracle updates written onchain for contract use | Offchain price computation and aggregation |
| 🎮 Game State | High-stakes state such as real-money outcomes | Low-stakes layers such as leaderboards and UI state |

This distinction is especially relevant for Ethereum-based ecosystems. Ethereum does not replace every function of the online stack; rather, it provides a neutral trust layer for coordination across actors who may not know or trust one another. In practice, this means a project may use conventional web infrastructure for front-end interaction, offchain computation, indexing, and content delivery, while anchoring treasury logic, governance, attestations, asset ownership, and other durable commitments on Ethereum. Some categories remain mixed by design: reputation scores may be computed offchain but anchored onchain through hashes or attestations; activity feeds are usually generated by offchain indexers that read onchain events; oracle-based price data is computed externally but written onchain for contract use; and game systems often split between high-stakes state onchain and low-stakes interface layers offchain.

For public-goods-oriented systems, this hybrid model is particularly powerful. Public goods require openness, broad accessibility, and mechanisms that reduce exclusion while sustaining collective maintenance. Web2 tools help distribute information and lower participation barriers, whereas Web3 tools help encode transparent rules, coordinate incentives, and create shared digital infrastructure that no single actor can unilaterally capture. Ethereum's role here is foundational: as a programmable public blockchain, it enables not only financial activity but also the institutional design of digital commons, from DAOs and attestations to funding mechanisms and interoperable identity layers. In that sense, the online/onchain relationship is not a competition between stacks but a division of labor between cloud-based service layers and Ethereum-based trust infrastructure.

## 3. Ethereum as the Core Infrastructure of the Onchain Paradigm
Ethereum is central to the relationship between Web3 and the onchain paradigm because it transformed blockchain from a narrow transactional system into a general-purpose environment for computation and coordination. Through smart contracts, Ethereum allows developers to define rules for ownership, exchange, access control, governance, and collective funding directly at the protocol layer. This capability distinguishes onchain systems from ordinary online services, where the rules are typically hidden inside platform databases and backend code.

From the standpoint of digital public infrastructure, Ethereum can be understood as a shared execution environment for applications that need credible neutrality, composability, and open participation. ENS supports human-readable identity; DeFi protocols enable permissionless financial primitives; DAOs organize governance and treasury management; attestation systems allow verifiable claims and reputation; and Layer 2 networks extend Ethereum's coordination model to more scalable environments while inheriting security guarantees from the base layer. Together, these elements make Ethereum a foundational substrate for Web3 systems that seek to align ownership, transparency, and collaboration.

For this reason, the distinction between **online** and **onchain** should not be interpreted as a simple replacement of one paradigm by another. Rather, it marks a reconfiguration of the internet in which cloud-based systems continue to support performance and usability, while Ethereum and related Web3 infrastructures provide shared trust, programmable ownership, and open coordination. The long-term significance of Web3 lies not only in decentralization as an abstract principle, but in the ability to build digital institutions whose rules, assets, and records remain accessible beyond the boundaries of any single platform.
